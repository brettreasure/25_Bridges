# 25 Bridges — Voice & Tone

**Dignified · Restrained · Modest**

The voice is steady. It tells you what we did, who it was for, and what it cost. It doesn't perform. It doesn't beg. It doesn't speak *about* the children when it could speak *with* them.

Christian language is a quiet thread, not a banner. We write so that someone of any faith — or none — can read our work without friction, and so that our church partners recognise their values in how we operate.

---

## A. Four Principles

These govern every sentence. Lower number wins when they conflict.

1. **Dignity before persuasion.** The children we work with are not characters in a fundraising story. If a sentence would be uncomfortable read aloud to the child it describes, it doesn't run.
2. **Modest before memorable.** Plain words, short sentences, specific facts. We don't reach for "transform", "empower", "unleash", or "change the world".
3. **Accurate before emotive.** Real numbers. Real names (with consent). Real timeframes. We never round to make a sentence feel bigger.
4. **Service before mission.** The Christian inheritance is one of service, neighbour, welcome. We name it where context requires it and let it sit quietly the rest of the time.

---

## B. Voice Attributes

| We are | We aren't |
|---|---|
| Steady & plain | Punchy or breathless |
| Specific & named | Generic or abstract |
| Humble & local | Heroic or global |
| Restrained & honest | Urgent or guilt-driven |
| Quietly faithful | Evangelical or preachy |
| Editorial & considered | Marketing-led |

- Use periods, not exclamation marks.
- Name names: "Amira, 9, in Mae La" — not "a child in a refugee camp".
- "Our tutor walked three kilometres" — not "an unstoppable hero on the frontlines".
- Donate buttons say "Donate" and the cost of what the donation funds — not "Save a life now".

## C. Tone Contexts

Voice stays constant. Tone shifts within a narrow band.

| Context | Register | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Editorial / story | Most quiet | "For most of the twentieth century the river ran behind the town like a backlot." |
| Programme update | Considered | "Forty-two students completed the reading programme this term. Twelve are new." |
| Donor appeal | Warm but factual | "A$25 funds a month of weekly tutoring for one child. A$300 funds a year." |
| Child story | Closer / direct | "Amira is nine. She arrived in Mae La in 2023. Her favourite letter is M." |
| UI & system | Plain, practical | "Donation received. Thank you. A receipt has been emailed to you." |

## D. Vocabulary

### Use
- **child / student** — plain; "student" frames them as a learner, not a recipient
- **family** — whole-person, not "case"
- **support** — replaces "help"; equal-footing
- **tutor / partner** — names a role; avoids "volunteer hero" framing
- **programme** — specific noun: "reading programme", "online tutoring programme"
- **donate / fund** — plain; don't decorate it; "A$25 funds…"
- **welcome / neighbour** — quiet faith vocabulary; warmth without preaching
- **service** — the Christian inheritance, in one word
- **care** — used sparingly; better than "love" in operational copy
- **walked / built / read / wrote** — concrete past-tense verbs of doing

### Avoid
- **rescue / save** — saviour framing; frames children as helpless
- **empower** — hollow buzzword; say what we actually did
- **transform / unleash** — marketing-speak; almost always overstated
- **change the world** — a cliché; untrue in any specific sense
- **the needy / the poor** — categorises people as conditions
- **heart-breaking / tragic** — emotive framing; let the facts carry the weight
- **unstoppable / heroic** — performative; we do not need this register
- **just A$X** — diminishes the gift; say the amount plainly
- **orphans / refugees** — use only where accurate; default to "children"
- **crusade / mission field** — colonial overtones; even in church-facing copy
- **!! / ??? / ALL CAPS** — no urgency typography; calm is the brand

## E. Copy Patterns

### Headlines — statement, not slogan
Complete sentence with a verb, ending in a full stop. Often a quiet detail rather than a grand claim.

**Pattern:** `[Specific subject] [past-tense verb] [outcome].`

> Forty-two students completed the reading programme *this term.*

### Ledes — one sentence, one fact
The most concrete fact in the piece. Begin with a year or a number.

> The online tutoring programme began in March 2023 with twelve students from Mae La. By April 2026 it had supported forty-eight, across three camps and four time zones.

### CTAs — verb & the cost
Two-part structure: the verb, then in mono below, what the gift funds.

**Pattern:** `[Verb] [amount]` + *(in mono)* `funds [specific thing] for [specific person/period]`

> **Donate A$300**
> *funds one year of weekly tutoring for one child*

Never "Just A$X". Never "Donate now!!".

### Microcopy — calm, complete sentences
UI strings are real sentences. Errors say what's wrong and how to fix it.

- Success: "Donation received. Thank you. A receipt has been emailed to you."
- Error: "We couldn't process this card. Check the number and try again, or use a different card."

No "Oops!". No exclamations. No emoji.

## F. Faith Language — Three Levels

Most public surfaces sit at Level 1.

| Level | Context | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Default public | Website, social, all public copy | "Our work is rooted in a tradition of service — welcoming neighbours and caring for children." |
| 2 — Church-partner | Church communications, faith-based grants | "We work as a Christian organisation… our approach reflects the call to welcome the stranger." |
| 3 — Devotional | Prayer letters, sermons-by-invitation | "Thank you for praying with us this quarter. Please hold Amira and her family in prayer…" |

## G. Guardrails

### Do
- Name names. Amira, 9. Mae La. February 2026. The reader trusts specificity.
- Past tense for impact. "Forty-two completed the term." Not "We are transforming education."
- State the cost, plainly. "A$25 funds a month of tutoring." Not "Just A$25".
- Use the children's own words when consent allows. Quoted in italic serif.
- Thank by being useful: receipts, updates, photos — not gushing.
- Read aloud. If it sounds like a sales pitch when spoken, rewrite.

### Don't
- No urgency typography. No exclamation marks, no all-caps, no "act now".
- No saviour framing. Donors don't "save" children; they support a programme.
- No diminutives. "Little", "tiny", "precious" — children are not props.
- No emoji in official copy. None.
- No evangelising on public surfaces. Faith is in how we behave.
- No round numbers we can't defend. If we say 42, it's 42.
- No "you" as guilt. "Without you, children will…" is the line we don't cross.
